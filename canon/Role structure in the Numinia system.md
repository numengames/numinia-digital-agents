The purpose of this document is to establish a structure for the cultural units of Numinia that is computable, quantifiable, and differential, insofar as the set of these units starts from a fictional (imaginary) characterization in order to project itself into an operational (pragmatic) situation. Put another way; the narrative elements of Numinia acquire true dimension (and meaning) at the moment they are reverted into the framework of work, thus constituting an operative and functional model.

The need to establish an indissoluble relationship between both concepts, narrative and social, is what leads us to begin from the fictional denomination of the original object: Numinia, and not from its social denomination: Numen Games; as a principle, it is necessary to understand that these two domains are not homologous, since they are not defined by the same attributions. Nevertheless, in some way they establish analogical relations, since both seem to represent the referent toward which their respective environments point: narrative and social. On the other hand, in this context Numinia is referring to its generic meaning, as a linguistic entity that names the game from which a narrative and rules are proposed as basic principles of understanding and action, and not, now, to its specific meaning within the narrative itself as the city-state within which the action of the game unfolds.

Our idea is that the game, with its narrative and its rules, is not a mere instrument or vehicle for particularizing a framework of work, but rather entails, in the semiotic terms of Peirce, a sort of ground, the referent toward which that framework of work points in order to describe itself, define itself, and recognize itself. It therefore constitutes a model, no longer prototypical, but archetypal, since, at bottom, its purpose is to rescue a common spatio-temporal context, situations and atavistic, universal sociocultural behaviors.

Then, in the same way that, as has been observed both in Jungian analytical psychology and in cultural anthropology, the archetype turns to myth, to fantasy, in order to become conscious, thereby defining that myth-fantasy as an archetypal model indispensable for its manifestation, our universal context requires a game, a regulated narrative in order to become conscious of itself.

The game, then, is not utilitarian, nor does it represent a reality alien to the object of which it is the referent, but rather it is meaningful, an indissociable characterization of said object (since it constitutes its model), and, above all, functional. This is something we will insist upon when we speak of the concept of function.

Can that operative framework, however, stand and develop without its interpretant, without its fantastic referent that is the game? Of course, and that is how it has been for a long time; but we are convinced that without said referent the operative framework proceeds in a much flatter way, with a substantially smaller dimension, and its possibilities are, therefore, far fewer.

On the other hand, we must not overlook the idea that the narrative, the fantastic referential object, ends up appearing in an overlapping and unconscious way (and, therefore, involuntary) in many of those operative frameworks, responding, precisely, to the need to dimension them. In these cases, however, that referential model is composed of sporadic brushstrokes and of which there is no real awareness; they appear out of necessity, but without awareness, without depth, and without consistency. Therefore, it is much more productive to establish this relationship in a premeditated and conscious manner, and, by understanding the significance and relevance possessed by the fantastic, fictional, narrative model of the game, to give all possible dimension to our context.

Finally, the operative model to which we aspire takes place in an abstract environment, between the digital world and the physical world, related to web 3.0 and the metaverse, in which relations with the fictional prove not only much closer and more propitious, but also much more productive.

Once the foundation of the relationship between the game and the operative framework has been laid out, as well as the indispensable reference to the former, it becomes necessary to build the structure of Numinia's types as a computable model, since its purpose is to project itself into an operative framework, and, therefore, into a pragmatic perspective.

To do so, we are going to take as our starting point the role system proposed by the systems thinking of the EEM Institute, both in its conception and in its nomenclature, as well as some basic foundations of semantics, such as prototype theory or frame semantics, which will be indispensable for categorizing and systematizing our structure, since the composition of said structure rests, ultimately, on the way in which the different types are signified within the structure, both in their intensional perspective and in their extensional perspective, and as functional entities within a pragmatic framework.

## Categorial relationship of semantic principles

Indeed, one of the great scientific values of semantics is that it is fully categorial, and, therefore, susceptible to functioning as a computable system. The relationships between the different entities of reality established by semantics are formed from opposition: an entity X is equal to X (X = X) one and only because it is opposed, in some of its inherent features (proper to its nature), to an entity Z that is equal to Z (Z = Z), with which, on the other hand, it may share category. Thus, for X = X automatically implies that X ≠ Z.

Likewise, categories constitute specific paradigms, among which there is also a relation of opposition. We may say, for example, that we have two phonological categories, which are vowels and consonants, and we know that the former are defined by having no obstacles in the vocal tract in the passage of air, and the latter by having some obstacle in the vocal tract in the passage of air. One category is opposed to the other, and is recognized precisely through this opposition (from which we understand, then, that there is an absolute dependence between both categories).

On the other hand, we have two consonantal phonemes, /p/ and /b/, which are defined by their articulation, and between them there exist both degrees of similarity and an inevitable relation of opposition: both are plosives and bilabials (subcategories), but the voiceless character of the phoneme /p/ irremissibly excludes the voiced character of the phoneme /b/. A phoneme cannot be voiceless and voiced at the same time, and we recognize a voiceless phoneme only insofar as voiced phonemes exist, for otherwise we would fall into an absurd tautology: /p/ is voiceless because it is voiceless, instead of recognizing the logic of its phonological features: /p/ is voiceless because it is not voiced.

This is pure basic logic, and it constitutes the basic foundation through which we understand, semantically, our reality: some features, by their condition, are opposed to other features, but it is precisely this opposition that helps us understand each feature. And these features are what define the character of an entity, as well as the category to which it belongs.

This semantic relation, of course, is not only applicable to linguistic entities, but to every sociocultural unit that forms part of a structure. This is expressed by Umberto Eco, who, from semiotics, has managed to particularize so accurately the relations between language, sign, and culture:

> [A cultural unit] is defined as a "place" in a system of other cultural units that oppose it and circumscribe it. A cultural unit subsists and is recognized insofar as there exists another that has a different value. It is the relation between various terms of a system of cultural units that deprives each one of them of what the others contribute (Eco, The Absent Structure).

Therefore, those cultural units are not only recognized by reason of their inherent features, which establish the opposition between them, but they need one another, for some possess those features of which the others lack. Dependence is, then, absolute, both on the epistemological plane, from which they are recognized and signified on the basis of their opposition, and on the pragmatic plane, from which they are needed in order to function. That is, they depend on one another. We will recall this quality of dependence when we recover the concept of function.

## Basic level theory

From this categorial character of semantics, the psychologist Eleanor Rosch, specialized in studies of cognitive linguistics, develops one of the most relevant aspects of basic semantics. Rosch observes, in the categorial system, a vertical dimension, in a relation of hyponymy and hypernymy between entities, and a horizontal dimension, which is based on relations of co-hyponymy.

Thus, the vertical dimension shows the way in which the elements of the same category are broken down, and how this develops in a relation of inclusion. Some units contain others, in a hierarchical system of categories and subcategories. Rosch fundamentally distinguishes three levels: a superordinate or general level; a basic level contained within the former; and a subordinate or specific level, in turn contained within the basic one. In her classic example, the superordinate level is animal, within which we find a subcategory of the basic level, which is bird; this subcategory is broken down, finally, into other smaller subcategories of the subordinate or specific level, one of which could be magpie.

This constitutes Basic Level Theory, so called because Rosch understands that the superordinate or general category is not effective in an operative system because it alludes to entities of very different order and only describes generic features; nor is the subordinate or specific category effective, since its specificity makes it less appropriate for the functioning of the cognitive system. The basic level, for its part, would be the most operative level, since, due to its informative efficacy, it alludes to easily recognizable realities and through which we can establish a more direct understanding of reality. It is at this level that the lexical terms that first appear in learning are situated.

The vertical dimension of Basic Level Theory offers us a first approximation to the types of Numinia: we recognize in this dimension the hierarchical categorization of the guilds, related to the occupation of each member of the community, insofar as it alludes to their training and knowledge, and which function in three levels (in relations of hyponymy and hypernymy): the guild proper (constituent), which contains two branches, each of which contains two houses. The constituent guild corresponds to the superordinate or general level; within it are contained two branches, each of which would be situated at a basic level (and, if both are hyponymous terms of the constituent guild, they also establish a relation of co-hyponymy between them); and each branch contains two houses, belonging to the subordinate or specific level (with the same relations of hyponymy with respect to the branch to which they belong and of co-hyponymy between them). Thus, the constituent guild is a hypernym of the branches, and each branch is a hypernym of the houses:

```
GUILD
├── Branch A
│   ├── House A.1
│   └── House A.2
└── Branch B
    ├── House B.1
    └── House B.2
```

The enormous relevance of this description of the guilds lies in understanding that all the entities of each guild, whatever level they may be at, belong to the same category. The constituent guild, the branch, and the house are not different categories, related by opposition, but hierarchical entities of a single category. Their signification is based on the degree of specification of their features. Thus, moreover, it is understood that the most effective reference in this sense is going to be the branch, since the constituent guild is too general, and the house, too specific (which is why the flow in the first contact with Numinia references us to a branch, although the constituent guild may seem a more appropriate perspective, solely because of its generic character).

At the same time, if we have a category called guilds, in a broad sense, within this category we find four entities, which define each of the four guilds of Numinia, and which are, in turn, subcategories that are indeed defined by opposition with a relation of co-hyponymy: they do not contain one another, but coexist in a horizontal relation. These four guilds are: alchemists, procurators, exegetes, and sentinels, which constitute four categories with their own hierarchies, each of which is defined by opposition to the others; they function, among themselves, in a horizontal and categorial dimension; and each one functions, on its own, in a vertical and hierarchical dimension.

It is absolutely relevant to understand that the fact that these four guilds form categories related by opposition in order to signify and recognize one another does not imply, in any way, that a member of the community cannot be reflected in two guilds at once. The opposition, in this case, refers to the semantic characterization of the category, independently of the relation of proximity that the individual establishes with these categories (in the same way that piloting and sailing are two attributions that are related by opposition; despite this, an individual may show great faculties both in piloting an airplane and in sailing a boat, although, of course, they will never be able to pilot an airplane and sail a boat at the same time).

In any case, this horizontal dimension of the elements leads us to Prototype Theory, within which we will characterize the other great group of Numinia: the factions.

## Prototype theory

We have already seen that the four categories of the guilds are related along a horizontal dimension, equal with respect to their functional character, while retaining values of opposition (and without forgetting the mutual need and dependence that exist within that opposition).

This helps us understand the relationship among the four main domains of Numinia, which we call factions, each associated with a field of development:

| Faction | Field of Development |
|---|---|
| **Heirs of Eleusis** | Play |
| **Stellar Circle** | Framework |
| **Hermeticists** | Education |
| **Neo-Atlantists** | Art |

First of all, we must not understand that there is a horizontal relationship between the guilds and the factions, for this would force us to understand that they are co-hyponyms, categories of the same hypernym and with some shared features. But this is not the case at all. Guilds and factions are cultural units of a very different order, which do not operate through a horizontal or vertical relationship, but a transversal one. They are not parallel paths or homologous entities, but transversal concepts that open up the multidimensional character of Numinia.

Like different typologies of a role-playing game, guilds and factions respond to very different contexts which nevertheless converge, inevitably, in the same reality of the operative framework. In an abstract, theoretical sense, both cultural units could remain distant, but in practice they converge in order to sustain the reality of Numinia, as will be seen.

If the guilds allude to occupations by reason of knowledge, the factions or domains correspond to different fields of development. Not of knowledge, since various kinds of knowledge fit within and are put into practice in each of these domains. They are different fertile lands that need to be cultivated with all kinds of forms of knowledge.

Although, in principle, the four aforementioned factions exist in Numinia, it should be noted that the horizontal categorial relationship is clearly characterized in three of them: play, the framework, and education. Art, for its part, proceeds along a less regulated path, and can establish much more open relationships with any of the other three fields of development, since both its teleological and axiological perspective and its functional character possess certain particularities in relation to the other domains that set it apart from that categorial relational system (these questions regarding the values, purposes, and functionalities of art point to a study of another order, which does not correspond to this context and, due to its extent and complexity, could not be accommodated within it). We do not set aside the domain of art as a faction; we simply free it from its presence within a categorial system of horizontal relations.

We therefore have three factions, which we observe as three different, horizontal subcategories and co-hyponyms within the category of Numinia's factions. Rosch defines this relationship as Prototype Theory; prototypical elements occupy a central place, while the other elements are peripheral. These prototypes are clearly related to the basic level, since, once again, it becomes much clearer to understand the reality of the world through them. They are also clearly representative of the category, establishing deeper relationships with each of the peripheral elements.

In this case, we could say that the prototype element of the category of Numinia's factions is play, not because it is the most important, but because it presents itself as the most characteristic and operative cultural unit. The framework and education, on either side of this central unit, are peripheral units; play always connects with them in one direction or another: if play always points toward the framework, or contains a greater or lesser degree of education, the framework always refers back to play, and education always takes reflections from it. It is much more difficult, however, to find the referents, as grounds, between the framework and education.

But this does not imply, in any way, that one of these factions is unimportant with respect to the other: the framework requires education, and education may revert into a framework. But the semantic and semiotic relations between both domains are not as clear as those established between play and the other two domains, whose referents, grounds, are recurrent and spontaneous cultural signs.

Moreover, this horizontal relationship, in which play is situated at the center, and the framework and education at the sides, greatly clarifies the functional dynamics: we always begin from play, from fiction as a ludic experience, which does not bring tensions and whose efforts are challenges as part of an adventure, in order to arrive at the social context: the framework and education.

The paths do not end there, and one may move from the framework to education and vice versa, but play is always an interference, even if it constitutes the beginning of the path and the other two domains appear to represent a later phase of it. This linear movement exists, although the dynamics are not closed and involve return paths or side-to-side movement. Even so, it helps us contemplate play as an initial, central, and intermediate phase along the path, whichever direction we travel it in. One may, of course, pass from the framework to education (or vice versa) without passing through play; but whoever does so will have abandoned the environment of Numinia, and the great dimension it provides will have disappeared.

Art, then, appears accompanying these domains, sometimes walking alongside them, other times contained within them; at others, as a means of expression of them...

## Approach to roles

As has been mentioned, our characterization of Numinia's roles is based on the role system of the EEM Institute, developed in the context of systems thinking. Its great contribution to the idea of role is its properly functional character. The metaphor of the theater serves to understand the difference between a subject (the performer) and the role (the character they perform).

From a purely operative plane, it is not much to say that the performer possesses a series of specific forms of knowledge, such as bodily expression, diction, verse, the Alexander Technique, etc., for none of that defines their role. These features, rather, define their training, what systems thinking calls their qualification (and in the context of Numinia we call profile). Their role is defined by the part they perform at a given moment, and this is their reality in an operative system. Therefore, the role is a figure that manifests itself in practice, at the moment when the subject takes charge of a function, the development of a task.

What we have concluded, however, is the complex way in which the performer accedes to that role, not now on the basis of their qualification, but of the re-signification they make of it, together with the intervention of their personal traits and the adoption of other aptitudes.

On the other hand, systems thinking defines position as the situation of the subject in a preferred role, whether because they have become stuck in it, having assumed this position unconsciously, or because, in a particular or circumstantial way, they have consciously chosen to assume this position. The position, as a preferred role, will not only mediate the action of the subject in the rest of the roles they perform, but will also place them in a more closed and static space.

Because, indeed, the role is, in view of its functional character, dynamic, while the position, which, although it is neither immovable nor irreversible, proves, as a preferred role, to be more stable, also suffers from greater statism. In the metaphor of the theater, different positions could be that of the leading man or the ingenue (the commedia dell'arte provided the history of theater with great positions, with what it came to call zanni and vecchi, or types: the capitano, the innamorata, the dottore, Colombina, Pantalone, Arlecchino, etc.). And in the context of Numinia, the position seems to establish a more direct relation with qualification.

For the time being, what is relevant for our structure of Numinia's types is to observe that none of the typologies observed up to this point (guilds or faction) fit the idea of role as a functional figure, as an entity that acquires signification and dimension, and, therefore, reality, in the practice of an operative framework; nevertheless, both categories will help us define, from their convergence, roles as living and dynamic figures of Numinia.

## Recovering the concept of function

It is very important to establish an idea regarding the functional character: function, as such, does not imply instrumentalization or utilitarianism; the concept of function is deeply related to the teleological and axiological planes of an entity, whether this be a cultural unit or a sign of that unit.

It is very different to conclude that an entity has certain purposes, values, and an end, than merely to affirm, arbitrarily, that it has a utility. The role system of the EEM Institute offers us a clear perspective on this with the example of the hammer and the microscope: if we merely accept that the hammer serves to drive nails and the microscope is an instrument for seeing that which is not perceptible to the naked eye, we will hardly be able to drive a nail when we lack a hammer, but have, on our desk, a microscope to which we have never given any use whatsoever. In the same way, the hammer may acquire other values and other ends, depending on the situation.

Thus, it is fundamental to recover that concept of function which, at a given moment, cognitive linguistics recovers in order to understand the way in which language functions in practice, from a pragmatic perspective. In this regard, the characterization that Louis Hjelmslev constructs of function from the semiotic model of glossematics is absolutely revealing, considering the importance that it had for the linguist to apply algebraic principles on the linguistic plane in order to constitute his semiotic model. Let us see what he says in this respect:

> We have adopted the term function in a sense that lies halfway between the logical-mathematical and the etymological (which has played so considerable a role in science, even in linguistic science), closer formally to the former but not identical with it. Precisely this intermediate and combinatory concept is what we need in linguistics. Thus, we may say that an identity of the text (or of the system) has certain functions, and by this think: first, approaching the logical-mathematical meaning, that the entity has dependency with other entities, in such a way that certain entities presuppose others; and second, approaching the etymological meaning, that the entity functions in a defined way, fulfills a defined role, takes a defined "position" in the chain (Hjelmslev, Prolegomena to a Theory of Language).

It is easy to recover that dynamic idea of function (and of role as a functional entity) in Hjelmslev's words, as a phenomenon in which the entity "fulfills a defined role," appealing, once again, to the pragmatic dimension (one cannot forget the enormous contribution to pragmatics of the semiotic model of glossematics). In fact, the linguist brings up the same term position to speak of a defined space taken by the entity (a term that for systems thinking defines a preferred role). But let us return to Hjelmslev's words in order to see his own and specific definition of function:

> We shall call function the dependency that satisfies the conditions of the analysis. Thus, we say that there is function between a class and its components (a chain and its parts, or a paradigm and its members) and between the components (parts or members) among themselves. We shall call the terminals of a function functives, understanding by functive an object that has function with other objects. It is said of it that it contracts its function (Hjelmslev, Prolegomena to a Theory of Language).

One must admire the enormous dimension of Hjelmslev's vision, who, from the construction of his semiotic model to be applied to language, constantly introduces suggestions and ideas for projecting his model beyond the linguistic sphere, foreseeing that it would be (as time has shown) extrapolable to other contexts and disciplines. It is thanks to that clear-sighted vision that his ideas contribute so much to our own context and to our structure.

We see, then, that function declares a relation of dependence, whether between entities (which for us may be cultural units) or between an entity and the paradigm that contains it. It is the same dependence that we observed between the different categories and semantic levels, for, after all, that dependence becomes evident at the moment when a pragmatic dimension is established; at the moment when the structure passes from being a theoretical model to a dynamic system within an operative process.

Thus, according to cognitive linguistics, language acquires reality in use, and function is the process that determines that reality. Function does not point to the character of an instrument nor to a theoretical utility, but to values and ends in the pragmatic dimension. And this, as was said before, is always motivated by a need. We may refer, in this regard, to the words of the Costa Rican linguist Carla Victoria Jara Murillo: "To believe that structure is prior to function is like believing that birds fly because some animals developed wings and then had to see what to do with them!"

## Roles, positions, and typologies in Numinia

Everything said above will help us, then, to build the structure of roles in the context of Numinia. We must consider the capital importance of the semantic character of the guilds, subject to Basic Level Theory, and as a categorial structure that functions, apparently, in a horizontal dimension, but within which four categories are characterized that stipulate relations of dependence and mutual need, and which function, individually, as four vertical and hierarchical substructures (each of them as a category).

And, on the other hand, the nature of the factions, a structure subject to Prototype Theory that functions in a purely horizontal dimension, with a central domain, two peripheral domains, and another itinerant domain (we may now recognize art as a domain subject to the aesthetic function, which, as regards its characterization in a sociocultural context, exhibits an eccentric behavior, without parallel, by virtue of its autonomous, immanent specificity and brought about by its reflexivity, being the only function of language that turns its gaze upon itself).

We have already concluded that the guilds fit what systems thinking calls qualifications, and that the different levels of each guild do not represent different categories, but more general or more specific descriptions of the same category:

**Alchemists** -- Superordinate level
- **Branch A: Artisans** -- Basic level
  - House A.1: Project designers -- Subordinate level
  - House A.2: Aesthetes -- Subordinate level
- **Branch B: Engineers** -- Basic level
  - House B.1: Architects -- Subordinate level
  - House B.2: Automata -- Subordinate level

**Exegetes** -- Superordinate level
- **Branch A: Chroniclers** -- Basic level
  - House A.1: Logographers -- Subordinate level
  - House A.2: Bards -- Subordinate level
- **Branch B: Erudites** -- Basic level
  - House B.1: Thaumaturges -- Subordinate level
  - House B.2: Hierophants -- Subordinate level

**Procurators** -- Superordinate level
- **Branch A: Legates** -- Basic level
  - House A.1: Legal rabbits -- Subordinate level
  - House A.2: Heralds -- Subordinate level
- **Branch B: Syndics** -- Basic level
  - House B.1: Mercurials -- Subordinate level
  - House B.2: Stewards -- Subordinate level

**Sentinels** -- Superordinate level
- **Branch A: Seraphim** -- Basic level
  - House A.1: Captains -- Subordinate level
  - House A.2: Guardians -- Subordinate level
- **Branch B: Archangels** -- Basic level
  - House B.1: Healers -- Subordinate level
  - House B.2: Pathfinders -- Subordinate level

Thus, we must confront the vertical structure of the guilds (their first horizontal characterization does not respond to the prototype model, but to a pure relation of co-hyponymy) with the horizontal structure of the fields of development, the factions. And if the superordinate level of each guild allows us to understand its generic character and its basic level helps us to understand its reality better and more directly, in this case the subordinate level will be what will serve us to confront each of the factions. Thus, each subordinate level of each member, particularized in each of the factions, will define a concrete role, being the result of a deep and specific level of qualification fulfilling a function within the context of action represented by each field of development:

| Domain | Position Type |
|---|---|
| **ART** | Itinerant position |
| **FRAMEWORK** | Peripheral position |
| **GAME** | Prototype |
| **EDUCATION** | Peripheral position |

For their part, positions will be defined in relation to the same operative process, as the product of the action resulting from putting into practice a series of qualifications in a field of development, but not necessarily delimited by the subordinate level, but rather driven by the basic level. That preferred role, taken consciously, constitutes, according to systems thinking, a referent for the rest of the individuals.

For, although it entails a more static and stable type, and its adoption involves a preferred form that mediates the behavior of other roles developed by the individual who takes said position, it also has, for that reason, a more predictable and recognizable character, and the other individuals know what they can expect from that position, and will be able to resort to it according to its character. It is, then, a very important figure for the community, although, due to the function it plays within the structure, it is more advisable that these positions be acquired by non-player characters (NPCs), subjects created by the fictional narrative, so that they may serve as anchors for the individuals of the community, player characters (PCs).

The choice of a position on the part of a PC should be punctual and highly meditated, for although, as has been seen, said position is not immovable, it does have a more consistent character, and is therefore more difficult to abandon. On the other hand, being more closed, it will not only mediate the action of other roles, but may even prevent the adoption of some of these roles.
